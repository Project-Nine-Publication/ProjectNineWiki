History

The Astorbridge family, known for their early 20th-century success in the regional steel industry, established the orphanage as a charitable endeavor during a period of rapid industrial growth. Founding matriarch Elizabeth Astorbridge believed strongly in community investment, setting aside a portion of her family’s land and wealth to support vulnerable children.

  • Early Years (1918–1950): Operated as the “Astorbridge Orphan Asylum,” housing up to 60 children in large dormitories. Strict routines were common, reflecting the institutional standards of the time.
  • Mid-Century Reforms (1950–1980): Progressive child welfare policies led to the introduction of smaller living units, on-site education, and social work support. During this period, the orphanage’s name was changed to Astorbridge Children’s Home to highlight its shift toward a family-like environment.
  • Modernization (1980–Present): A series of renovations added therapy spaces, modern dorm rooms, and updated recreational facilities. The Astorbridge Foundation continues to finance expansions, ensuring the Home remains a leader in child-focused residential care.

    • Built in 1918 in a Georgian Revival style with a red-brick façade, white columns, and a prominent central tower.
    • The front foyer features original hardwood floors, a grand staircase, and a commemorative plaque honoring Elizabeth Astorbridge.
    • Upper floors have been partially converted into administrative offices, a library, and private bedrooms for older teens and live-in staff.

  2. Residential Wings

  3. Multipurpose Hall & Chapel

  4. Outdoor Spaces

Programs and Services

  • Residential Care: Astorbridge provides full-time housing for children and adolescents, emphasizing a stable, supportive environment that mirrors a family setting.
  • Therapy & Counseling: Licensed social workers and therapists offer individual and group counseling, addressing mental health, trauma, and social-emotional development.
